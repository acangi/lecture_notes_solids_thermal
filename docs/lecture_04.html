4.2 From Lecture 3 to Symmetry

In Lecture 3 we described a crystal in real space as a Bravais lattice plus basis. Today we add the next layer: beyond translations, crystals may also be invariant under rotations, reflections, inversion, or combinations of these.

A symmetry operation is a geometrical transformation that leaves the crystal indistinguishable from itself. If at least one point remains fixed, the allowed symmetry operations form a point group.

For crystal physics this distinction is essential:

  • the Bravais lattice captures translational symmetry,
  • the point group captures rotational and reflection symmetry,
  • the basis may reduce the symmetry of the decorated crystal relative to the empty Bravais lattice.

Thus observable properties are constrained by the actual point group of the crystal, not just by the translational skeleton.

4.3 Point Symmetries and the Crystallographic Restriction

4.3.1 Point Groups in Crystals

For finite figures, many rotational symmetries are possible. For periodic crystals, translational symmetry imposes a strong additional restriction: not every rotation can coexist with a lattice.

In Hermann–Mauguin notation, examples of crystallographic point groups are

  • \(m\bar 3m\) for cubic symmetry,
  • \(4/mmm\) for tetragonal symmetry,
  • \(6/mmm\) for hexagonal symmetry,
  • \(2/m\) for monoclinic symmetry.

There are \(32\) crystallographic point groups in three dimensions, but we do not need their full catalog today. What matters first is why only certain rotation orders are allowed.

4.3.2 Derivation: Why Only \(1\)-, \(2\)-, \(3\)-, \(4\)-, and \(6\)-Fold Rotations Survive

Consider first a two-dimensional lattice, or equivalently a plane perpendicular to a candidate rotation axis in three dimensions. Let \(\mathbf a\) be the shortest lattice vector in that plane, and suppose rotation by an angle \(\phi\) is a lattice symmetry.

Then the rotated vectors \(\mathcal R(\phi)\mathbf a\) and \(\mathcal R(-\phi)\mathbf a\) are also lattice vectors. Their sum is therefore a lattice vector as well:

\[ \mathcal R(\phi)\mathbf a + \mathcal R(-\phi)\mathbf a = 2 \cos\phi \mathbf a . \]

Because this vector is parallel to the shortest lattice vector \(\mathbf a\), the coefficient \(2\cos\phi\) must be an integer compatible with a lattice vector parallel to \(\mathbf a\):

\[ 2\cos\phi \in {2,1,0,-1,-2}. \]

Hence the only allowed angles are

\[ \phi = 0,\ \pm \frac{\pi}{3},\ \pm \frac{\pi}{2},\ \pm \frac{2\pi}{3},\ \pi . \]

So periodic crystals allow only rotation orders

\[ n = 1,2,3,4,6 . \]

This is the crystallographic restriction.

4.3.3 Interpretation

  1. Fivefold symmetry is excluded in periodic crystals. This is why ordinary Bravais lattices cannot have a fivefold axis.

  2. The restriction comes from compatibility with translation, not from rotation alone. A regular pentagon is perfectly symmetric as a finite figure, but periodic repetition with exact lattice translations fails.

  3. The same logic underlies the organization into crystal systems. Cubic, tetragonal, trigonal, hexagonal, orthorhombic, monoclinic, and triclinic systems are distinguished by which crystallographic symmetries are present.

4.4 Why Point Symmetry Matters Physically

4.4.1 Neumann’s Principle

A foundational statement of crystal physics is Neumann’s principle:

The symmetry elements of any observable physical property of a crystal must include the symmetry elements of the crystal’s point group.

Equivalently, any tensor describing a macroscopic property must be invariant under the crystal’s point-group operations.

4.4.2 Consequence for Tensors

This immediately reduces the number of independent tensor components.

For a second-rank tensor \(T_{ij}\):

  • in a cubic crystal, symmetry forces the three diagonal entries to be equal and the off-diagonal entries to vanish, so \[ T_{ij} = T \delta_{ij} ; \]
  • in a tetragonal crystal, the basal plane is equivalent but the \(c\) axis is distinct, so the most general diagonal form is \[ T_{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} T_\perp & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & T_\perp & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & T_\parallel \end{pmatrix}. \]

Thus cubic symmetry makes a second-rank property effectively isotropic, while tetragonal symmetry still allows anisotropy between in-plane and out-of-plane directions.

4.4.3 Consequence for Selection Rules

Symmetry also constrains transitions. If an external perturbation does not transform compatibly with the initial and final states, the corresponding matrix element vanishes. This is the origin of selection rules.

At the level needed today:

  • point symmetry constrains which tensor couplings or optical transitions are allowed,
  • translational symmetry constrains how wave vectors may change in scattering processes.

The translational version will reappear below in reciprocal space.

4.5 The Reciprocal Lattice

4.5.1 Definition

The direct lattice is generated by the primitive vectors \(\mathbf a_1\), \(\mathbf a_2\), and \(\mathbf a_3\). The reciprocal lattice is generated by vectors \(\mathbf b_1\), \(\mathbf b_2\), and \(\mathbf b_3\) defined by

\[ \mathbf b_i \cdot \mathbf a_j = 2\pi \delta_{ij}. \]

From this definition one obtains

\[ \mathbf b_1 = 2\pi \frac{\mathbf a_2 \times \mathbf a_3} {\mathbf a_1 \cdot (\mathbf a_2 \times \mathbf a_3)}, \qquad \mathbf b_2 = 2\pi \frac{\mathbf a_3 \times \mathbf a_1} {\mathbf a_1 \cdot (\mathbf a_2 \times \mathbf a_3)}, \qquad \mathbf b_3 = 2\pi \frac{\mathbf a_1 \times \mathbf a_2} {\mathbf a_1 \cdot (\mathbf a_2 \times \mathbf a_3)}. \]

Any reciprocal-lattice vector is then

\[ \mathbf G = h \mathbf b_1 + k \mathbf b_2 + l \mathbf b_3, \qquad h,k,l \in \mathbb Z. \]

4.5.2 Immediate Properties

If \(\mathbf R_n = n_1 \mathbf a_1 + n_2 \mathbf a_2 + n_3 \mathbf a_3\) is a direct-lattice vector, then

\[ \mathbf G \cdot \mathbf R_n = 2\pi (n_1 h + n_2 k + n_3 l), \]

so

\[ e^{i\mathbf G \cdot \mathbf R_n}=1. \]

This simple identity is one of the most useful relations in solid-state physics.

4.5.3 Why This Matters

If a function is lattice periodic,

\[ F(\mathbf r + \mathbf R_n)=F(\mathbf r), \]

then its Fourier expansion contains only reciprocal-lattice vectors:

\[ F(\mathbf r)=\sum_{\mathbf G} F_{\mathbf G} e^{i\mathbf G\cdot \mathbf r}. \]

So the reciprocal lattice is not a mathematical curiosity. It is the natural index set for any lattice-periodic quantity.

4.5.4 Sanity Check: Simple Cubic Lattice

For a simple cubic direct lattice,

\[ \mathbf a_1 = a \hat{\mathbf x}, \qquad \mathbf a_2 = a \hat{\mathbf y}, \qquad \mathbf a_3 = a \hat{\mathbf z}, \]

the reciprocal vectors are

\[ \mathbf b_1 = \frac{2\pi}{a}\hat{\mathbf x}, \qquad \mathbf b_2 = \frac{2\pi}{a}\hat{\mathbf y}, \qquad \mathbf b_3 = \frac{2\pi}{a}\hat{\mathbf z}. \]

So a simple cubic lattice maps to another simple cubic lattice, but with lattice constant \(2\pi/a\) in reciprocal space.

This is a useful check on dimensions and normalization.

4.6 Reciprocal Space and Crystal Planes

4.6.1 Reciprocal Vectors as Normals to Lattice Planes

For the family of direct-lattice planes with Miller indices \((hkl)\), define

\[ \mathbf G_{hkl}=h\mathbf b_1 + k\mathbf b_2 + l\mathbf b_3. \]

This vector is normal to the planes labeled by \((hkl)\).

This is one of the main reasons reciprocal space is so useful: crystallographic planes, which are awkward to describe directly in a non-orthogonal basis, become simple normal vectors in reciprocal space.

4.6.2 Derivation of the Plane Spacing Formula

Let \(d_{hkl}\) be the spacing between neighboring planes in the \((hkl)\) family. Since \(\mathbf G_{hkl}\) is normal to these planes, the plane spacing is the projection of the displacement between two adjacent planes onto the normal direction. The result is

\[ d_{hkl} = \frac{2\pi}{|\mathbf G_{hkl}|}. \]

4.6.3 Interpretation

  1. Large Miller indices mean closely spaced planes. Increasing \(|\mathbf G_{hkl}|\) decreases \(d_{hkl}\).

  2. The reciprocal lattice is the natural language for diffraction. Plane spacings and plane normals become simple vector statements.

  3. The same geometry later controls phonon and electron dispersions. High-symmetry directions in the Brillouin zone are reciprocal-space descendants of real-space crystal symmetry.

4.6.4 Example: Simple Cubic Interplanar Spacing

For the simple cubic case,

\[ \mathbf G_{hkl} = \frac{2\pi}{a} \left( h\hat{\mathbf x} + k\hat{\mathbf y} + l\hat{\mathbf z} \right), \]

so

\[ |\mathbf G_{hkl}| = \frac{2\pi}{a}\sqrt{h^2+k^2+l^2}, \]

and therefore

\[ d_{hkl} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{h^2+k^2+l^2}}. \]

This familiar formula is just the special cubic version of the general reciprocal-space relation.

4.7 Brillouin Zones and Equivalent Wave Vectors

4.7.1 The First Brillouin Zone

The first Brillouin zone is the Wigner–Seitz cell of the reciprocal lattice.

This definition mirrors the Wigner–Seitz construction in direct space:

  • direct lattice \(\rightarrow\) Wigner–Seitz cell around a lattice point,
  • reciprocal lattice \(\rightarrow\) Brillouin zone around \(\mathbf G=\mathbf 0\).

The first Brillouin zone is preferred over an arbitrary reciprocal primitive parallelepiped because it makes the symmetry of the crystal manifest.

4.7.2 Bloch Labeling

For a lattice-periodic Hamiltonian, eigenstates can be labeled by a wave vector \(\mathbf k\) such that

\[ \psi_{\mathbf k}(\mathbf r + \mathbf R_n) = e^{i\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf R_n} \psi_{\mathbf k}(\mathbf r). \]

This is the essential content of Bloch’s theorem.

Because

\[ e^{i(\mathbf k+\mathbf G)\cdot \mathbf R_n} = e^{i\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf R_n} e^{i\mathbf G\cdot \mathbf R_n} = e^{i\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf R_n}, \]

the wave vectors \(\mathbf k\) and \(\mathbf k+\mathbf G\) are equivalent. Therefore physically distinct states may be labeled by choosing \(\mathbf k\) inside a single primitive region, conventionally the first Brillouin zone.

4.7.3 Symmetry of the Dispersion

If the crystal has a point-group operation \(\mathcal R\), then the dispersion satisfies

\[ \varepsilon(\mathcal R \mathbf k)=\varepsilon(\mathbf k). \]

In addition, reciprocal-lattice periodicity gives

\[ \varepsilon(\mathbf k+\mathbf G)=\varepsilon(\mathbf k). \]

So the full dispersion is determined by two facts:

  1. it is periodic in reciprocal space;
  2. it inherits the crystal’s point-group symmetry.

This is why one usually plots only:

  • the first Brillouin zone, and often even only
  • an irreducible wedge or a high-symmetry path inside it.

4.7.4 Why Dispersion Is Plotted in \(\mathbf k\)-Space

For a lattice-periodic system, \(\mathbf k\) is the natural label of translational behavior. Writing the dispersion as \(\varepsilon(\mathbf k)\) or \(\omega(\mathbf k)\) is not merely conventional; it is the most compact way of encoding:

  • lattice periodicity,
  • crystal symmetry,
  • degeneracies at symmetry-related points,
  • and how modes vary from one translational pattern to another.

In contrast, plotting directly in real space would hide the translational classification entirely.

4.8 Translational Selection Rules in Reciprocal Space

If an elementary process occurs in a perfect crystal, the crystal wave vector is conserved only modulo a reciprocal-lattice vector.

For example, when an electron absorbs a phonon of wave vector \(\mathbf q\),

\[ \mathbf k' = \mathbf k + \mathbf q + \mathbf G. \]

For phonon emission,

\[ \mathbf k = \mathbf k' + \mathbf q + \mathbf G. \]

Processes with \(\mathbf G=\mathbf 0\) are called normal processes, and those with \(\mathbf G\neq \mathbf 0\) are umklapp processes.

This is the translational version of a selection rule. It is one of the main reasons reciprocal space is indispensable once we discuss phonons, transport, and scattering.

4.9 Bridge to Later Lectures: Phonon Branches and Density of States

The reciprocal-space language developed today is exactly what later lattice dynamics requires.

4.9.1 Why Branches Live in \(\mathbf k\)-Space

If the primitive cell contains \(p\) atoms, then each \(\mathbf k\) carries \(3p\) vibrational degrees of freedom. Later these will appear as branches

\[ \omega_s(\mathbf k), \qquad s=1,\dots,3p. \]

For every \(\mathbf k\) in the first Brillouin zone, the crystal can support a finite set of normal modes. Plotting the frequencies against \(\mathbf k\) therefore produces the phonon dispersion relation.

4.9.2 Why the First Brillouin Zone Is Enough

Because \(\mathbf k\) and \(\mathbf k+\mathbf G\) are equivalent, one needs only the first Brillouin zone to represent all distinct translational patterns. High-symmetry lines inside that zone are chosen because they reveal degeneracies, splittings, and branch connectivity most clearly.

4.9.3 Why the Density of States Is Also a Reciprocal-Space Object

The phonon density of states is obtained by counting how many states in the full Brillouin zone fall into a small frequency interval. Thus:

  • the dispersion tells us which frequencies occur,
  • the Brillouin-zone counting tells us how many states occur at each frequency.

Flat regions of a dispersion contribute strongly because many nearby \(\mathbf k\) values correspond to nearly the same frequency.

4.9.4 Conceptual Summary of the Bridge

Today’s logic is the prerequisite for later phonon lectures:

  1. direct lattice \(\rightarrow\) reciprocal lattice,
  2. reciprocal lattice \(\rightarrow\) Brillouin zone,
  3. Brillouin zone \(\rightarrow\) \(\mathbf k\)-dependent branches,
  4. full zone counting \(\rightarrow\) density of states.

Without the reciprocal-space framework, neither phonon dispersions nor density-of-states arguments can be formulated cleanly.

4.10 Take-Home Messages

NoteTake-Home Messages
  • Periodic crystals admit only the rotation orders compatible with translational symmetry, namely one-, two-, three-, four-, and sixfold rotations.
  • The point group of a crystal is the symmetry structure relevant for physical tensors and selection rules.
  • Neumann’s principle explains why symmetry reduces the number of independent material parameters.
  • The reciprocal lattice is defined so that lattice periodicity becomes simple in Fourier space.
  • Reciprocal-lattice vectors encode plane normals and interplanar spacings in a compact geometric form.
  • The first Brillouin zone is the symmetric fundamental region of reciprocal space.
  • Bloch states are naturally labeled by wave vectors that are equivalent up to reciprocal-lattice translations.
  • Dispersion relations are plotted in \(\mathbf k\)-space because \(\mathbf k\) is the natural label of lattice-periodic eigenstates.
  • Later phonon branches and density-of-states calculations are direct consequences of the reciprocal-space viewpoint introduced today.
