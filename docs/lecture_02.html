2.2 Why Bonding Enters Thermal Physics

Electrons are the “glue” that holds atomic cores together in solids. Depending on how valence electrons are redistributed, localized, or delocalized, the solid acquires a different type of bond and therefore a different cohesive energy and mechanical rigidity.

A solid exists because the total energy has a minimum at a finite interatomic distance. The cohesive energy is the energy by which the solid state lies below the energy of the separated free atoms or molecules:

\[ E_{\mathrm{coh}} = E_{\mathrm{free\ atoms}} - E_{\mathrm{solid}} > 0 . \]

A larger \(E_{\mathrm{coh}}\) usually signals stronger bonding, but for thermal physics the shape of the minimum matters just as much as its depth. A broad, shallow minimum and a narrow, steep minimum may have similar depths while leading to very different vibrational frequencies.

For van der Waals bonded solids, a common model is the Lennard-Jones potential,

\[ U_{\mathrm{LJ}}(r) = 4\varepsilon \left[ \left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12} - \left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^6 \right] , \]

which makes the competition between attraction and short-range repulsion explicit.

For ionic crystals, the natural starting point is instead the long-range Coulomb energy of the entire ionic lattice. If site \(\mathbf R_i\) is occupied by an ion of charge \(Z_i e\), the electrostatic contribution is

\[ E_{\mathrm M} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i\neq j} \frac{Z_i Z_j e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 |\mathbf R_i-\mathbf R_j|} . \]

This lattice sum is called the Madelung energy. It is not a nearest-neighbor model: because the Coulomb interaction decays only as \(1/r\), distant ions contribute appreciably, and the geometry of the entire crystal enters through the Madelung constant \(\alpha\). For a binary ionic crystal, the electrostatic energy per chemical unit is commonly written as

\[ \frac{E_{\mathrm M}}{N} = -\alpha \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r} , \]

where \(r\) is the nearest-neighbor cation–anion distance. The value of \(\alpha\) depends on the structure; for example, the rock-salt and cesium-chloride lattices have different Madelung constants.

The Madelung term captures the dominant attraction, but it cannot by itself explain the stability of an ionic crystal. Since it scales as \(-1/r\), it would favor an unphysical collapse of the ions toward one another. A second ingredient is therefore required: short-range repulsion when closed electron shells begin to overlap. Microscopically this is tied to the Pauli principle and the rapid increase of energy when the electronic clouds are compressed.

For ionic solids, this repulsive part is often modeled by the Born-Mayer approximation, which replaces the Lennard-Jones-type hard-core term by an exponential:

\[ U_{\mathrm{ion}}(r) = \alpha \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r} - B \exp\left(-\frac{r}{\rho}\right) . \]

Here \(B\) sets the scale of the repulsion and \(\rho\) its range. The equilibrium spacing \(r_0\) follows from the minimum condition

\[ \left.\frac{dU_{\mathrm{ion}}}{dr}\right|_{r=r_0} = \alpha \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_0^2} - \frac{B}{\rho}\exp\left(-\frac{r_0}{\rho}\right) = 0 . \]

This is the point at which the long-range Coulomb attraction and the short-range core repulsion balance.

This form is already enough to see why bonding matters for thermal physics. Once a minimum exists at \(r_0\), the local curvature

\[ K_{\mathrm{bond}} = \left. \frac{d^2 U_{\mathrm{ion}}}{dr^2} \right|_{r=r_0} \]

defines the stiffness of the bond against small displacements. A steeper minimum means a larger restoring force, a larger characteristic vibrational frequency, and therefore a larger vibrational energy scale. In ionic crystals, the balance of Madelung attraction and Born-Mayer repulsion is thus the microscopic origin of both the equilibrium spacing and the stiffness that later enters lattice dynamics and the Debye temperature.

2.3 Periodic Table Trends and the Main Bond Types

2.3.1 Reading the Periodic Table for Bonding

As atoms are brought together, their electronic levels split and broaden. Deep core levels remain nearly atomic, while the outer valence states overlap strongly and are responsible for bonding. The nature of that overlap determines the bond type.

The periodic table already suggests the dominant bonding mechanism:

  • similar electronegativities + local orbital overlap \(\rightarrow\) often covalent bonding,
  • large electronegativity difference \(\rightarrow\) often ionic bonding,
  • spatially extended valence states and partially filled bands \(\rightarrow\) metallic bonding,
  • closed shells or saturated molecules \(\rightarrow\) only weak van der Waals attraction remains,
  • hydrogen bound to strongly electronegative atoms can produce hydrogen bonding.

2.3.2 Covalent Bonding

In covalent bonding, electrons are concentrated between neighboring atoms. This gives a strongly directional bond, so bond angles matter in addition to bond lengths. Examples are diamond, Si, and Ge, where directional bonding is associated with tetrahedral coordination and, for group-IV elements, with \(sp^3\) hybridization.

A useful periodic trend appears already within group IV: the bond energies decrease from C to Si to Ge, reflecting reduced overlap and increasing atomic size down the group.

2.3.3 Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonding is favored when one atom gives up electrons relatively easily and another gains them relatively easily. In practice, a large electronegativity difference \(\Delta \chi\) is the relevant indicator. The resulting crystal is held together primarily by Coulomb attraction between oppositely charged ions, stabilized at short distance by strong repulsion of overlapping closed shells.

The key energetic quantity for ideal ionic crystals is the Madelung energy, which sums the long-range Coulomb interactions over the whole crystal.

2.3.4 Metallic Bonding

In metals, the valence electrons are not localized in individual two-center bonds. Instead, they are spread over many atoms. Because the valence states are spatially extended, the bond is much less directional than in covalent solids, and dense packing becomes structurally important.

This delocalization is also why metallic solids are not only bonded, but electrically and thermally conductive in a way that covalent and ionic insulators are not.

2.3.5 Hydrogen Bonding

A hydrogen bond forms when hydrogen, already bound to one atom, also interacts attractively with a second atom, typically one that is strongly electronegative. It is weaker than ordinary covalent or ionic bonding, but much stronger than a typical van der Waals interaction.

In solids, hydrogen bonds are especially important in ice and in biological materials.

2.3.6 Van der Waals Bonding

Van der Waals bonding is the weak attraction between closed-shell atoms or saturated molecules caused by fluctuating and induced dipoles. Its attractive part decays rapidly with distance, as \(r^{-6}\).

This is the characteristic bond of noble-gas solids and many molecular crystals.

2.3.7 Typical Energy Scales

  • Ge: about \(1.95,\mathrm{eV}\) per atom,

  • noble-gas solids: only about \(0.1,\mathrm{eV}\) per atom in order of magnitude.

2.4 From the Bonding Curve to Stiffness

2.4.1 Equilibrium Distance and Curvature

Let \(U(r)\) be an effective interatomic potential for a given bond. The equilibrium distance \(r_0\) is defined by

\[ \left.\frac{dU}{dr}\right|_{r=r_0}=0 . \]

Near equilibrium, the Taylor expansion is

\[ U(r) = U(r_0) + \frac{1}{2} K_{\mathrm{bond}}(r-r_0)^2 + \mathcal O\left((r-r_0)^3\right), \]

\[ K_{\mathrm{bond}} = \left.\frac{d^2U}{dr^2}\right|_{r=r_0}. \]

This second derivative is the local bond stiffness. It is the simplest quantitative bridge from bonding to vibrations.

2.4.2 Why Curvature, Not Only Depth, Matters

The depth \(|U(r_0)|\) and the curvature \(K_{\mathrm{bond}}\) are related but not identical.

  • A deep minimum means that it costs a lot of energy to separate atoms completely.
  • A steep minimum means that even a small displacement away from equilibrium costs a lot of energy.

Thermal vibrations probe the local shape of the minimum, so they depend more directly on \(K_{\mathrm{bond}}\) than on the full dissociation energy alone.

This is the reason a thermal-physics course must go beyond “strong bond versus weak bond.” For vibrations, what matters first is the restoring force for small displacements.

2.4.3 Local Vibrational Frequency Scale

For two atoms with reduced mass \(\mu\), define the relative displacement

\[ x = r-r_0 . \]

In the harmonic approximation,

\[ U(r) \approx U(r_0) + \frac{1}{2}K_{\mathrm{bond}} x^2 . \]

The equation of motion for the relative coordinate is then

\[ \mu \ddot x = -K_{\mathrm{bond}} x , \]

\[ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{K_{\mathrm{bond}}}{\mu}} . \]

Thus the characteristic vibrational scale increases with:

  • larger curvature \(K_{\mathrm{bond}}\),
  • smaller mass \(\mu\).

This is the central physical message of the lecture.

2.4.4 Interpretation

The formula

\[ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{K_{\mathrm{bond}}}{\mu}} \]

passes two immediate checks:

  1. Weak molecular crystal: small \(K_{\mathrm{bond}}\) and often heavy molecules \(\rightarrow\) low-frequency vibrations.
  2. Light-element covalent network: large \(K_{\mathrm{bond}}\) and small mass \(\rightarrow\) high-frequency vibrations.

This is why light, strongly covalent solids are natural candidates for large phonon energies, while van der Waals solids are not.

Within the group-IV series, both trends work in the same direction: going from C to Si to Ge, the bond energy decreases while the mass increases, so the vibrational scale should decrease strongly down the group.

2.5 From Local Stiffness to the Debye Scale

A crystal does not vibrate as isolated pairs; it vibrates collectively. Still, the pair picture captures the right scaling. In a lattice, the second derivatives of the total energy with respect to atomic displacements become the force constants. These determine the normal-mode frequencies of the crystal.

For an acoustic branch, the long-wavelength modes are characterized by a sound velocity \(v_s\). Dimensional reasoning gives

\[ v_s \sim a \sqrt{\frac{K_{\mathrm{bond}}}{M}}, \]

where \(a\) is a characteristic spacing and \(M\) is an atomic mass scale. Since the Debye cutoff frequency scales like

\[ \omega_D \sim \frac{v_s}{a}, \]

one obtains the qualitative relation

\[ k_B \Theta_D = \hbar \omega_D, \qquad \Theta_D \propto \sqrt{\frac{K_{\mathrm{bond}}}{M}} . \]

The omitted prefactors depend on structure and dimensionality, but the trend is robust: stiffer bonds and lighter atoms push the Debye temperature upward.

2.5.1 Interpretation

This scaling explains, at a qualitative level, why:

  • light covalent solids tend to have high Debye temperatures,
  • many ionic and partly covalent ceramics also have comparatively high vibrational scales,
  • heavy metals often have lower Debye temperatures than light covalent semiconductors,
  • molecular crystals have low Debye temperatures because their intermolecular forces are weak.

It is important not to over-interpret this. A high \(\Theta_D\) signals high characteristic phonon energies, but it does not by itself determine the thermal conductivity. That requires scattering physics, and in metals electronic carriers contribute in addition to phonons.

2.6 Materials Emphasis: Why Ceramics, Metals, and Molecular Crystals Differ Thermally

2.6.1 Ceramics

Many ceramics are dominantly ionic or mixed ionic-covalent solids. Because their bonds are strong, their equilibrium potentials are usually steep near the minimum. As a result, they often have large elastic moduli and comparatively high phonon energy scales.

From a thermal viewpoint, this means:

  • high characteristic vibrational frequencies,
  • often relatively high Debye temperatures,
  • but not automatically high thermal conductivity, because complex structures and disorder can still scatter phonons strongly.

2.6.2 Metals

Metals are bonded by delocalized electrons. Their vibrational stiffness is often moderate rather than extreme, but their thermal behavior is special because electrons carry both charge and heat.

Therefore:

  • the lattice part of the thermal scale is not necessarily exceptional,
  • yet the total thermal conductivity can be large because of electronic transport,
  • and the specific heat acquires both lattice and electronic contributions.

2.6.3 Molecular Crystals

Molecular crystals are held together mainly by van der Waals forces and sometimes supplemented by hydrogen bonds. Their cohesive energies are much smaller than those of ionic or covalent solids.

Hence they are typically:

  • soft,
  • low-melting,
  • rich in low-frequency vibrations,
  • low in Debye temperature,
  • poor thermal conductors.

2.6.4 A Useful Mixed Case: Graphite

Bonding can differ strongly by direction. In graphite, the bonding is covalent within the layers but only van der Waals between layers.

This immediately implies strong thermal anisotropy:

  • stiff, high-frequency motion and efficient heat flow are favored in-plane,
  • much softer response is expected perpendicular to the layers.

This example is a good warning against speaking of “the” stiffness of a solid without specifying direction.

Optional / Appendix: Microscopic Models

For ionic solids, the important deeper result is that the Coulomb energy must be summed over the whole crystal, leading to the Madelung constant.

For covalent solids, the Heitler-London and LCAO pictures are often useful for interpretation, where bonding and antibonding states emerge from combining atomic orbitals. The key thermal takeaway is simply that the bonding state concentrates charge between atoms and thereby creates a strong, directional restoring force.

For hybridization, especially \(sp^3\) in diamond-like solids, the main point is structural: bond directionality is built into the electronic wavefunctions themselves.

These microscopic constructions will become useful again later when we discuss why some crystals are especially stiff, anisotropic, or semiconducting.

2.7 Bridge to Lattice Dynamics and the Next Lecture

Today we stayed at the level of bonding curves and characteristic scales. The next lecture replaces the single-coordinate picture \(U(r)\) by the many-atom displacement field of a crystal.

The logical chain is:

  1. Choose equilibrium atomic positions.
  2. Expand the total crystal energy for small displacements.
  3. Keep the quadratic terms: these are the force constants.
  4. Solve for the collective normal modes.
  5. Interpret the resulting spectrum as phonons.
  6. Build from that spectrum the density of states and, later, the heat capacity.

In compact form,

\[ U({ \mathbf u_\ell }) \longrightarrow \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial u_{\ell \alpha} \partial u_{\ell' \beta}} \longrightarrow \omega_\nu(\mathbf q) \longrightarrow D(\omega) \longrightarrow C_V . \]

The point of today’s lecture is that the entire chain begins with chemistry: bond type sets stiffness, stiffness sets vibrational scales, and vibrational scales set thermal scales.

NoteTake-Home Messages
  • The periodic table already contains strong clues about which bonding mechanism a solid will prefer.
  • Covalent bonds are directional, ionic bonds are dominated by Coulomb attraction, metallic bonds are delocalized, and van der Waals bonds are weak induced-dipole attractions.
  • Typical bonding energies span orders of magnitude, from molecular-crystal scales of order \(0.1 \mathrm{eV}\) to several eV for ionic and covalent solids.
  • The thermal relevance of a bond is not set only by the depth of the energy minimum, but also by its curvature near equilibrium.
  • The local bond stiffness is the second derivative of the interatomic potential at the minimum.
  • Characteristic vibrational frequencies increase with increasing stiffness and decreasing atomic mass.
  • The Debye temperature is a vibrational energy scale, so it tends to be high in light, stiff solids and low in soft, weakly bonded solids.
  • Thermal conductivity is not determined by stiffness alone: scattering and, in metals, electronic heat transport matter as well.

2.8 Problem Set

  1. Bond Classification from the Periodic Table. For each of the following solids, identify the dominant bonding type: solid Ar, ice, NaCl, Cu, and Si. Then rank them qualitatively from lowest to highest expected characteristic vibrational scale.

  2. From the Potential Minimum to a Vibrational Frequency. Let \(U(r)\) have a minimum at \(r_0\). Show that the harmonic expansion around \(r_0\) leads to a simple-harmonic-oscillator equation for the relative displacement \(x=r-r_0\), and derive the corresponding angular frequency in terms of \(K_{\mathrm{bond}}\) and the reduced mass \(\mu\).

  3. Trend Within Group IV. Assuming representative cohesive energies of about \(7.3,\mathrm{eV}\) per atom for diamond, \(4.64,\mathrm{eV}\) per atom for Si, and \(3.87,\mathrm{eV}\) per atom for Ge. Use these data together with periodic-table trends to explain why the characteristic phonon scale decreases from C to Si to Ge.

  4. Directional Bonding and Thermal Anisotropy. Graphite has strong covalent bonding within the layers and weak van der Waals bonding between layers. Explain qualitatively how this should affect the elastic response and the thermal transport parallel and perpendicular to the layers.

  5. Why Metals Are Thermally Special. A metal and an ionic crystal may have comparable order-of-magnitude cohesive energies. Explain why their thermal conductivities can nevertheless be very different.
