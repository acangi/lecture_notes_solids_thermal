7.2 From General Formalism to Explicit Chains

In Lecture 6 we reduced harmonic lattice dynamics to an eigenvalue problem for the dynamical matrix. Based on this, we work through the simplest explicit models analytically. The reward is that several abstract concepts from the previous lecture become concrete:

  • Brillouin-zone restriction,
  • dispersion relations,
  • group velocity,
  • acoustic versus optical branches,
  • and the counting argument that leads directly to a density of states.

7.3 Monatomic Linear Chain

We begin with a one-dimensional chain of identical atoms of mass \(M\), equally spaced by the lattice constant \(a\). Only longitudinal displacements are kept, and only nearest-neighbor couplings are retained.

7.3.1 Model and Harmonic Energy

Let the equilibrium position of atom \(n\) be \[ x_n^{(0)} = n a, \] and let \(u_n(t)\) denote its displacement from equilibrium. The harmonic potential energy is \[ U = \frac{K}{2}\sum_n \left(u_{n+1}-u_n\right)^2. \]

This is the one-dimensional specialization of the harmonic lattice energy from Lecture 6: only relative displacements matter, and only nearest neighbors are coupled.

7.3.2 Equation of Motion

Differentiating the potential with respect to \(u_n\) gives \[ M\ddot u_n = -K\left(2u_n-u_{n-1}-u_{n+1}\right). \]

This is the discrete wave equation for the monatomic chain.

\[ M\ddot u_n + K\left(2u_n-u_{n-1}-u_{n+1}\right)=0. \]

7.3.3 Born–von Kármán Boundary Conditions and Allowed \(q\)-Values

To keep the algebra simple, we impose periodic boundary conditions: \[ u_{n+N}(t)=u_n(t). \]

We then seek plane-wave solutions of the form \[ u_n(t)=u(q)e^{i(qna-\omega t)}. \]

The boundary condition gives \[ e^{iqNa}=1, \] hence \[ q = \frac{2\pi j}{Na}, \qquad j\in\mathbb Z. \]

However, \(q\) and \(q+G\) with reciprocal-lattice vector \[ G=\frac{2\pi}{a} \] describe the same displacement pattern on the discrete lattice sites, since \[ e^{i(q+G)na}=e^{iqna}e^{i2\pi n}=e^{iqna}. \]

Therefore only \(N\) distinct wavevectors exist, and they may be chosen inside the first Brillouin zone: \[ -\frac{\pi}{a}<q\le \frac{\pi}{a}. \]

\[ q_j=\frac{2\pi j}{L}, \qquad L=Na, \qquad -\frac{\pi}{a}<q\le \frac{\pi}{a}. \]

7.3.4 Dispersion Relation

Substituting the plane-wave ansatz into the equation of motion yields \[ -M\omega^2 u(q) = -K\left(2-e^{-iqa}-e^{iqa}\right)u(q) = -2K\left(1-\cos qa\right)u(q). \]

For nonzero amplitude, \[ \omega^2(q)=\frac{2K}{M}\left(1-\cos qa\right). \]

Equivalently,

\[ \omega(q)=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\left|\sin\frac{qa}{2}\right|. \]

This is the dispersion relation of the monatomic linear chain.

7.3.5 Long-Wavelength Limit and Sound Speed

For \(|qa|\ll 1\), \[ \sin\frac{qa}{2}\approx \frac{qa}{2}, \] so the dispersion becomes linear: \[ \omega(q)\approx a\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}} |q|. \]

Thus the chain behaves as an elastic continuum in the long-wavelength limit. The slope at the zone center is the sound speed, \[ v_s = a\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}. \]

This is why the branch is called acoustic.

7.3.6 Group Velocity

The group velocity of a wave packet is \[ v_g(q)=\frac{d\omega}{dq}. \]

For \(0\le q\le \pi/a\), where the absolute value may be dropped, \[ \omega(q)=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\sin\frac{qa}{2}, \] so \[ v_g(q)=a\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\cos\frac{qa}{2}. \]

Hence \[ v_g(0)=v_s, \qquad v_g \left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)=0. \]

The vanishing of \(v_g\) at the zone boundary means that the dispersion flattens there: zone-edge modes do not transport energy through the chain as a wave packet.

\[ v_g(q)=\frac{d\omega}{dq} = a\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\cos\frac{qa}{2} \qquad (0\le q\le \pi/a). \]

7.3.7 Interpretation

  • \(\omega(q)=\omega(-q)\), as expected from left-right symmetry and time-reversal invariance.
  • \(\omega(0)=0\): a rigid translation of the chain costs no energy.
  • The maximum frequency occurs at \(|q|=\pi/a\): \[ \omega_{\max}=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}. \]
  • In the ideal 1D chain, only longitudinal modes are supported in the harmonic approximation; transverse restoring forces are absent in this model.

7.4 Diatomic Linear Chain

We now place two atoms in each primitive cell: one of mass \(M_1\) at position \(na\), and one of mass \(M_2\) at position \(na+a/2\). Let their displacements in cell \(n\) be \(u_n(t)\) and \(v_n(t)\), respectively. As before, only nearest-neighbor couplings and longitudinal motion are retained.

7.4.1 Harmonic Energy and Equations of Motion

The harmonic potential energy is \[ U = \frac{K}{2}\sum_n (u_n-v_n)^2 + \frac{K}{2}\sum_n (v_n-u_{n+1})^2. \]

The equations of motion are therefore \[ M_1\ddot u_n = -K(2u_n-v_n-v_{n-1}), \] \[ M_2\ddot v_n = -K(2v_n-u_n-u_{n+1}). \]

7.4.2 Plane-Wave Ansatz and \(2\times 2\) Eigenvalue Problem

We use \[ u_n(t)=u(q)e^{i(qna-\omega t)}, \qquad v_n(t)=v(q)e^{i(qna-\omega t)}. \]

This gives \[ -\omega^2 M_1 u = -2Ku + K(1+e^{-iqa})v, \] \[ -\omega^2 M_2 v = -2Kv + K(1+e^{iqa})u. \]

Equivalently, \[ \begin{pmatrix} 2K-M_1\omega^2 & -K(1+e^{-iqa}) \\ -K(1+e^{iqa}) & 2K-M_2\omega^2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} u\\ v \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0\\ 0 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Nontrivial solutions exist only if the determinant vanishes.

7.4.3 Acoustic and Optical Branches

Setting the determinant to zero yields two branches:

\[ \omega_\pm^2(q) = \frac{K}{M_1M_2} \left[ (M_1+M_2) \pm \sqrt{(M_1+M_2)^2-4M_1M_2\sin^2\frac{qa}{2}} \right]. \]

There are now two frequencies for each allowed \(q\), because the primitive cell contains two dynamical degrees of freedom along the chain.

7.4.4 Zone Center: In-Phase and Out-of-Phase Motion

At \(q=0\), the off-diagonal coupling is maximal because \(1+e^{iqa}=2\). The amplitude ratio becomes \[ \frac{v(0)}{u(0)} = \begin{cases} +1, & \text{acoustic branch } \omega_-(q),\\ -\dfrac{M_1}{M_2}, & \text{optical branch } \omega_+(q). \end{cases} \]

So:

  • in the acoustic branch, the two atoms move in phase and the whole primitive cell is translated;
  • in the optical branch, the two atoms move against each other and the center of mass remains fixed.

Indeed, for the optical mode at \(q=0\), \[ M_1 u + M_2 v = 0. \]

The zone-center optical frequency is \[ \omega_+(0)=\sqrt{2K\left(\frac{1}{M_1}+\frac{1}{M_2}\right)}. \]

7.4.5 Long-Wavelength Acoustic Limit

Expanding the lower branch for \(|qa|\ll 1\) gives \[ \omega_-(q)\approx a|q|\sqrt{\frac{K}{2(M_1+M_2)}}. \]

Thus the acoustic branch is again linear near \(q=0\), but the effective inertia is now set by the total mass in the primitive cell.

7.4.6 Zone Boundary: Sublattice-Selective Motion

At the Brillouin-zone boundary, \(q=\pi/a\), one has \[ 1+e^{iqa}=1+e^{i\pi}=0, \] so the two amplitudes decouple. The allowed frequencies are therefore \[ \omega^2=\frac{2K}{M_1} \qquad\text{or}\qquad \omega^2=\frac{2K}{M_2}. \]

If \(M_1>M_2\), then

  • the lower-frequency branch is \[ \omega_-\left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)=\sqrt{\frac{2K}{M_1}}, \] and only the heavier sublattice moves;
  • the higher-frequency branch is \[ \omega_+\left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)=\sqrt{\frac{2K}{M_2}}, \] and only the lighter sublattice moves.

This is a very useful physical picture: at the zone edge, the normal modes can become localized on one sublattice.

7.4.7 Why the Terms “Acoustic” and “Optical” Make Sense

The lower branch is called acoustic because it behaves like a sound wave in the long-wavelength limit: neighboring cells oscillate almost in phase and \(\omega_-(q)\to 0\) as \(q\to 0\).

The upper branch is called optical because its frequency remains finite at \(q=0\) and, in ionic chains, the relative motion of oppositely charged sublattices produces a dipole moment that can couple to light.

7.4.8 Interpretation

  • Two atoms per primitive cell \(\rightarrow\) two longitudinal branches in this 1D model.
  • The acoustic branch always lies below the optical branch for unequal masses.
  • In the long-wavelength limit, the acoustic mode translates each cell almost rigidly.
  • The optical mode describes internal motion of the basis.

7.5 Bridge to the Next Lecture: From Allowed \(q\)-Values to a Density of States

The worked chains above do more than provide explicit dispersions. They also show how one passes from a discrete crystal to a continuum description in reciprocal space, which is the starting point for thermal calculations.

7.5.1 Step 1: Count the Allowed \(q\)-Values

For a chain of length \(L=Na\) with periodic boundary conditions, \[ q_j=\frac{2\pi j}{L}. \]

Hence the spacing between neighboring wavevectors is \[ \Delta q = \frac{2\pi}{L}. \]

So in the large-\(N\) limit, the number of states in an interval \(dq\) is \[ \frac{dq}{\Delta q}=\frac{L}{2\pi},dq. \]

For each branch, this is the fundamental counting rule that converts sums over discrete \(q\) into integrals: \[ \sum_q f(q) \longrightarrow \frac{L}{2\pi}\int_{\mathrm{BZ}} f(q),dq. \]

For an even dispersion, one often writes instead \[ \sum_q f(q) \longrightarrow \frac{L}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi/a} f(q),dq. \]

7.5.2 Step 2: Convert Mode Counting from \(q\) to \(\omega\)

Suppose a branch \(\lambda\) has dispersion \(\omega_\lambda(q)\). Then the density of states of that branch is defined by \[ g_\lambda(\omega)=\sum_q \delta \left(\omega-\omega_\lambda(q)\right). \]

In the continuum limit, \[ g_\lambda(\omega) = \frac{L}{2\pi} \sum_{q_i(\omega)} \left| \frac{d\omega_\lambda}{dq} \right|_{q_i}^{-1}, \] where the sum runs over all wavevectors \(q_i\) that satisfy \(\omega_\lambda(q_i)=\omega\).

Thus the density of states is controlled by the inverse slope of the dispersion. Equivalently, \[ g_\lambda(\omega)\propto \left|\frac{dq}{d\omega}\right|. \]

This is the central logic: \[ \boxed{\text{mode counting in }q} \rightarrow \boxed{\omega(q)} \rightarrow \boxed{g(\omega)}. \]

7.5.3 Step 3: Monatomic-Chain Example

For the monatomic chain, \[ \omega(q)=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\left|\sin\frac{qa}{2}\right|, \qquad \omega_{\max}=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}. \]

For \(0<\omega<\omega_{\max}\) there are two solutions, \(\pm q\), with \[ q(\omega)=\frac{2}{a}\arcsin!\left(\frac{\omega}{\omega_{\max}}\right). \]

Therefore \[ \left|\frac{dq}{d\omega}\right| = \frac{2}{a\omega_{\max}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-(\omega/\omega_{\max})^2}}, \] and the density of states becomes

\[ g(\omega) = \frac{2L}{\pi a\omega_{\max}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-(\omega/\omega_{\max})^2}} \qquad (0<\omega<\omega_{\max}). \]

The divergence at \(\omega=\omega_{\max}\) occurs because \[ v_g=\frac{d\omega}{dq}\to 0 \] at the Brillouin-zone edge. In one dimension this is the simplest example of a van Hove singularity.

7.5.4 What Carries Over to More General Lattices?

The same logic survives in higher dimensions and with multiple branches:

  1. count the allowed \(\mathbf q\) values,
  2. determine the dispersion \(\omega_\lambda(\mathbf q)\),
  3. convert the counting measure in \(\mathbf q\)-space into a density in frequency space,
  4. sum over branches.

This is the handoff to the next stage of the course: once the normal modes are quantized and a density of states is known, thermal quantities such as internal energy and heat capacity can be computed systematically.

7.6 Optional / Appendix: Equal-Mass Limit and Band Folding

Open optional appendix

If the two masses become equal, \(M_1=M_2=M\), the diatomic-chain result reduces to \[ \omega_\pm^2(q)=\frac{2K}{M}\left[1\pm \cos\frac{qa}{2}\right]. \]

Thus one may write \[ \omega_{\mathrm{ac}}(q)=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\left|\sin\frac{qa}{4}\right|, \qquad \omega_{\mathrm{op}}(q)=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{M}}\left|\cos\frac{qa}{4}\right|. \]

The apparent “optical” branch is then not a genuinely new branch. The true primitive cell has length \(a/2\), so the true Brillouin-zone edge is at \(2\pi/a\), not \(\pi/a\). In the smaller Brillouin zone associated with period \(a\), the monatomic dispersion is simply folded back and appears as two branches.

This is a useful reminder that branch counting must always be done with the smallest primitive cell.

NoteTake-Home Messages
  • The monatomic linear chain has one longitudinal acoustic branch with a finite Brillouin-zone cutoff.
  • Restricting \(q\) to the first Brillouin zone is not a loss of information; wavevectors that differ by a reciprocal-lattice vector represent the same lattice displacement pattern.
  • The long-wavelength part of the monatomic dispersion is linear and defines the sound speed.
  • The group velocity is the slope of the dispersion and vanishes at the zone boundary.
  • A diatomic primitive cell produces two branches: one acoustic and one optical.
  • At the zone center, the acoustic mode translates the cell, whereas the optical mode describes internal motion of the basis.
  • At the zone boundary of the diatomic chain, the normal modes can become confined to one sublattice.
  • The density of states follows from counting allowed \(q\)-values and transforming that counting through the dispersion relation.

7.7 Problem Set

  1. Monatomic chain: dispersion and sound speed. Starting from \[ U=\frac{K}{2}\sum_n (u_{n+1}-u_n)^2, \] derive the equation of motion of the monatomic chain, insert the plane-wave ansatz \[ u_n(t)=u(q)e^{i(qna-\omega t)}, \] and obtain the dispersion relation. Then determine the sound speed from the long-wavelength limit.

  2. Allowed wavevectors and Brillouin-zone restriction. For a monatomic chain of length \(L=Na\) with periodic boundary conditions, derive the allowed values of \(q\). Show explicitly that \(q\) and \(q+2\pi/a\) generate the same displacement pattern on the lattice sites, and explain why one may restrict \(q\) to the first Brillouin zone.

  3. Diatomic chain: branch structure and mode character. Starting from \[ U = \frac{K}{2}\sum_n (u_n-v_n)^2 + \frac{K}{2}\sum_n (v_n-u_{n+1})^2, \] derive the coupled equations of motion and the two branches \(\omega_\pm(q)\). Then determine the amplitude ratio \(v/u\) at \(q=0\) and explain the physical meaning of each branch.

  4. From dispersion to density of states. Use the allowed-\(q\) counting for the monatomic chain to derive the density of states \[ g(\omega) = \frac{L}{2\pi} \sum_{q_i(\omega)} \left| \frac{d\omega}{dq} \right|^{-1}_{q_i}. \] Evaluate this explicitly for the monatomic-chain dispersion and identify the frequency at which the density of states diverges.
