5.2 From Symmetry and Reciprocal Space to Diffraction

In Lecture 4 we introduced the reciprocal lattice, reciprocal-lattice vectors \(\mathbf G\), plane spacings \(d_{hkl}\), and the first Brillouin zone. Today these objects stop being purely geometrical and become directly measurable.

The central idea is simple:

  • the periodicity of the lattice selects special momentum transfers;
  • the basis inside the primitive cell weights those allowed transfers;
  • the measured diffraction pattern is therefore a reciprocal-space image of the crystal.

This is why diffraction is the experimental counterpart of the reciprocal-space formalism introduced in the previous lecture.

5.3 Elastic Diffraction from a Periodic Lattice

5.3.1 Bragg’s Geometric Picture

Suppose radiation of wavelength \(\lambda\) is reflected from a family of parallel lattice planes of spacing \(d_{hkl}\). Constructive interference occurs when the path difference between rays reflected from neighboring planes is an integer multiple of \(\lambda\).

\[ 2 d_{hkl}\sin\theta = m\lambda, \qquad m\in\mathbb Z. \]

This is the Bragg condition.

Interpretation:

  1. only certain scattering angles are allowed for a given wavelength;
  2. smaller plane spacing means larger scattering angle;
  3. higher-order peaks correspond to larger reciprocal vectors, or equivalently to larger integers \(m\).

5.3.2 Laue’s Reciprocal-Space Picture

Now describe the same process atom by atom. Let the incident beam have wave vector \(\mathbf k\) and the scattered beam \(\mathbf k'\). For two equivalent atoms separated by a lattice vector \(\mathbf R_n\), the phase difference is

\[ \Delta \phi = (\mathbf k-\mathbf k')\cdot \mathbf R_n. \]

Constructive interference must hold for every lattice vector \(\mathbf R_n\). Since reciprocal-lattice vectors satisfy \(\mathbf G\cdot \mathbf R_n = 2\pi \times \text{integer}\), the diffraction condition is

\[ \mathbf k-\mathbf k'=\mathbf G. \]

This is the Laue condition.

So the lattice does not allow arbitrary momentum transfer. In elastic diffraction, the scattering vector must be a reciprocal-lattice vector.

5.3.3 Equivalent Elastic Form

Because elastic scattering does not change the wavelength,

\[ |\mathbf k| = |\mathbf k'| = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}. \]

Squaring the Laue condition gives a useful reciprocal-space criterion:

\[ \mathbf k\cdot \mathbf G = \frac{1}{2} |\mathbf G|^2. \]

This says that diffraction occurs when the tip of the incident wave vector lies on a Bragg plane in reciprocal space.

5.3.4 Why Bragg and Laue Are the Same Statement

From Lecture 4 we know that reciprocal-lattice vectors normal to the \((hkl)\) planes satisfy

\[ |\mathbf G_{hkl}| = \frac{2\pi}{d_{hkl}} \]

for the primitive reciprocal vector normal to that plane family. More generally, order \(m\) corresponds to the reciprocal vector \(m\mathbf G_{hkl}\). Combining this with \(|\mathbf G| = 2|\mathbf k|\sin\theta\) yields the Bragg condition again:

\[ 2 d_{hkl}\sin\theta = m\lambda. \]

So Bragg’s construction in direct space and Laue’s construction in reciprocal space are mathematically equivalent.

5.3.5 Sanity Check

The Bravais lattice determines which momentum transfers are allowed:

  • if \(\mathbf Q \equiv \mathbf k-\mathbf k'\) is not a reciprocal-lattice vector, the scattered amplitudes cancel almost completely;
  • if \(\mathbf Q=\mathbf G\), a Bragg peak may appear.

At this stage we have located the allowed peaks, but we have not yet explained why some are strong, weak, or absent. That information comes from the basis.

5.4 From Bravais Lattice to Basis: Structure Factor

5.4.1 Total Scattering Amplitude

Let the primitive cell contain \(p\) atoms at positions \(\mathbf r_j\), and let atom \(j\) scatter with amplitude \(f_j(\mathbf Q)\). Then the total elastic scattering amplitude from the crystal is

\[ A(\mathbf Q) = \sum_n \sum_{j=1}^{p} f_j(\mathbf Q) e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot(\mathbf R_n+\mathbf r_j)}. \]

The sum naturally factorizes into a lattice part and a basis part:

\[ A(\mathbf Q) = \left( \sum_n e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf R_n} \right) \left( \sum_{j=1}^{p} f_j(\mathbf Q)e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf r_j} \right). \]

Define the structure factor of one primitive cell by

\[ F(\mathbf Q) = \sum_{j=1}^{p} f_j(\mathbf Q)e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf r_j}. \]

Then

\[ A(\mathbf Q) = \left( \sum_n e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf R_n} \right) F(\mathbf Q). \]

5.4.2 What the Two Factors Mean

The factorization is physically decisive:

  • \(\sum_n e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf R_n}\) encodes the Bravais lattice and produces the Laue selection rule \(\mathbf Q=\mathbf G\);
  • \(F(\mathbf G)\) encodes the basis and determines the intensity of the allowed Bragg peak.

For an ideal large crystal, the lattice sum is nonzero only at reciprocal-lattice vectors, so the measured Bragg intensity scales as

\[ I(\mathbf G)\propto |F(\mathbf G)|^2. \]

This is the single most important equation of the lecture: peak positions come from the lattice; peak intensities come from the basis.

5.4.3 Atomic Form Factor

For X-ray scattering, the single-atom amplitude is the Fourier transform of the electronic charge distribution of that atom. In course notation we write

\[ f_j(\mathbf Q) \propto \int \rho_{e,j}(\mathbf r),e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf r},d^3r. \]

This is the atomic form factor.

Consequences:

  1. X-rays and electrons primarily probe electron density.
  2. Heavier atoms often scatter more strongly in X-ray diffraction.
  3. The basis weights are not just geometrical phase factors; they also depend on atomic species.

For nonmagnetic neutron scattering, the role of \(f_j\) is played by the nuclear scattering length, which is much less directly tied to atomic number.

5.4.4 What If the Basis Is Monatomic?

If there is only one atom in the primitive cell at the origin, then

\[ F(\mathbf G)=f(\mathbf G). \]

All allowed peaks are then weighted only by the single-atom form factor. No extra phase cancellations occur inside the cell.

This is the baseline case against which basis effects are measured.

5.5 Reciprocal-Space Selection Rules and Systematic Extinctions

A reciprocal-lattice vector may satisfy the Laue condition and still give zero intensity because the basis contributions cancel. These are systematic extinctions.

5.5.1 Worked Example: Face-Centered Cubic Lattice

Index the structure with the conventional cubic cell of edge length \(a\). The four equivalent atomic positions are

\[ (0,0,0), \qquad \left(\frac12,\frac12,0\right), \qquad \left(\frac12,0,\frac12\right), \qquad \left(0,\frac12,\frac12\right). \]

For identical atoms, the structure factor is

\[ F_{\mathrm{fcc}}(hkl) = f \left[ 1 + e^{-i\pi(h+k)} + e^{-i\pi(h+l)} + e^{-i\pi(k+l)} \right]. \]

Now evaluate the parity cases:

  • if \(h,k,l\) are all even or all odd, every exponential equals \(+1\), so \[ F_{\mathrm{fcc}}(hkl)=4f; \]
  • if even and odd indices are mixed, the terms cancel and \[ F_{\mathrm{fcc}}(hkl)=0. \]

Thus the selection rule is:

For fcc diffraction, only reflections with Miller indices all even or all odd are allowed.

This is a genuine structural fingerprint.

5.5.2 Interpretation

It is tempting to say that certain reciprocal-lattice points are “missing.” The deeper statement is subtler:

  • if one works with the true primitive cell, the reciprocal lattice is the correct one from the start;
  • if one indexes with the conventional cubic cell, the extinction appears as cancellation inside a larger nonprimitive cell.

So systematic extinctions are not arbitrary missing data. They encode the translational arrangement of the basis.

5.5.3 Optional Extension: Diamond Structure

Diamond may be viewed as an fcc Bravais lattice plus a two-atom basis at

\[ \mathbf r_1 = \mathbf 0, \qquad \mathbf r_2 = \frac14(\mathbf a_1+\mathbf a_2+\mathbf a_3). \]

Then the extra basis factor is

\[ F_{\mathrm{diamond}}(hkl) = F_{\mathrm{fcc}}(hkl) \left[ 1+e^{-i\pi(h+k+l)/2} \right]. \]

Hence:

  • mixed parity is already forbidden by the fcc rule;
  • among all-even reflections, those with \(h+k+l = 4m+2\) are extinguished;
  • among all-odd reflections, the intensity is reduced relative to the strongest all-even family.

This is how diffraction distinguishes diamond from monatomic fcc structures.

5.6 What Experiments Actually Measure

5.6.1 The Ewald Construction

The reciprocal-space condition becomes visually transparent in the Ewald construction. Draw the incident wave vector \(\mathbf k\) with its tip on one reciprocal-lattice point, then draw a sphere of radius \(|\mathbf k|\) centered at the tail of \(\mathbf k\).

If another reciprocal-lattice point lies on that sphere, then a diffracted beam exists in the direction \(\mathbf k'\) from the sphere center to that point. Equivalently,

\[ \mathbf k-\mathbf k'=\mathbf G, \qquad |\mathbf k|=|\mathbf k'|. \]

So diffraction is experimentally observed when the Ewald sphere intersects the reciprocal lattice.

5.6.2 The Detector Does Not See the Crystal Directly

A diffraction experiment measures intensity as a function of scattering direction, and sometimes as a function of wavelength or sample orientation. In the simplest elastic setting:

  • the location of a Bragg peak determines the reciprocal-lattice vector \(\mathbf G\) and therefore the lattice geometry;
  • the intensity of that peak determines \(|F(\mathbf G)|^2\) and therefore constrains the basis;
  • the absence of a peak may be as informative as its presence.

5.6.3 The Phase Problem

If the complex amplitudes \(F(\mathbf G)\) were known, the lattice-periodic density could in principle be reconstructed by inverse Fourier transformation. In practice, standard diffraction measures intensities, not complex phases. Therefore one obtains

\[ I(\mathbf G)\propto |F(\mathbf G)|^2, \]

but not the phase of \(F(\mathbf G)\).

This is the phase problem of diffraction.

Interpretation:

  1. diffraction data strongly constrain the structure;
  2. they do not uniquely reveal it without additional assumptions or refinement methods;
  3. “what experiments actually measure” is therefore more limited than “the full microscopic density.”

5.6.4 Experimental Geometries in a Nutshell

There are the following classical ways to satisfy the diffraction condition:

  • the Laue method, using many wavelengths at fixed incident direction;
  • the rotating-crystal method, using a single crystal whose orientation is varied;
  • the powder method, using many grain orientations at once, which turns Bragg conditions into rings.

For this lecture’s purposes, all three are practical ways of sampling the same reciprocal-space condition.

5.6.5 Which Probe Tells You What?

The diffraction condition depends only on wavelength, but the probe–matter interaction determines what structural information is emphasized.

  • X-rays and electrons couple primarily to electron density.
  • Neutrons couple to nuclei and, when relevant, to magnetic moments.
  • Thermal neutrons naturally have wavelengths of order ångströms and energies of order \(0.1\ \mathrm{eV}\), which makes them especially useful once energy-resolved scattering is considered.

5.7 Bridge to the Next Lecture: From Elastic Diffraction to Inelastic Scattering

Elastic diffraction is powerful, but it is fundamentally a probe of the static periodic structure. In elastic scattering,

\[ \Delta E = 0, \qquad \mathbf Q = \mathbf k-\mathbf k'=\mathbf G. \]

So elastic Bragg peaks tell us about lattice periodicity and basis interference, but not directly about the excitation spectrum of the solid.

By contrast, in inelastic scattering one measures both momentum transfer and energy transfer:

\[ \mathbf Q = \mathbf k-\mathbf k', \qquad \hbar\omega = E-E'. \]

Now the scattered probe can create or absorb an excitation of the crystal. For lattice vibrations, the measured momentum transfer is decomposed as

\[ \mathbf Q = \mathbf G + \mathbf q, \]

where \(\mathbf q\) is the reduced wave vector in the first Brillouin zone. The corresponding energy transfer reveals the phonon frequency \(\omega_s(\mathbf q)\).

This is the route from reciprocal-space diffraction geometry to experimentally measured phonon dispersions: elastic diffraction identifies the reciprocal lattice, while inelastic scattering maps how excitations disperse around it.

Thermal neutrons are especially well suited to this task because their wavelengths match lattice spacings and their energies are comparable to typical solid-state excitation energies.

5.8 Take-Home Messages

NoteTake-Home Messages
  • Bragg’s law and the Laue condition are two equivalent ways of expressing elastic diffraction in a crystal.
  • The Bravais lattice determines the allowed peak positions in reciprocal space.
  • The basis determines the relative intensities of those peaks through the structure factor.
  • Systematic extinctions are structural information, not experimental accidents.
  • Reciprocal space becomes experimentally visible through the Ewald construction.
  • Standard diffraction measures intensities and angles, but not the phase of the complex scattering amplitude.
  • Different probes emphasize different microscopic constituents of the solid.
  • Elastic diffraction reveals static structure, while inelastic scattering is needed to determine phonon dispersions.

5.9 Problem Set

  1. Laue to Bragg. Starting from the phase difference between waves scattered by two equivalent atoms separated by a lattice vector \(\mathbf R_n\), derive the Laue condition. Then show how elastic scattering turns it into Bragg’s law for planes of spacing \(d_{hkl}\).

  2. Lattice Versus Basis. For a crystal with basis positions \(\mathbf r_j\) and atomic amplitudes \(f_j(\mathbf Q)\), derive the factorized scattering amplitude \[ A(\mathbf Q) = \left(\sum_n e^{-i\mathbf Q\cdot \mathbf R_n}\right) F(\mathbf Q). \] Explain clearly which factor determines peak positions and which factor determines peak intensities.

  3. fcc Extinction Rule. Using the four conventional-cell positions of an fcc lattice, derive \[ F_{\mathrm{fcc}}(hkl) = f \left[ 1 + e^{-i\pi(h+k)} + e^{-i\pi(h+l)} + e^{-i\pi(k+l)} \right]. \] Show that reflections occur only when \(h,k,l\) are all even or all odd.

  4. Diamond as an fcc Lattice Plus Basis. Starting from the fcc rule, add a second identical atom at \(\frac14(\mathbf a_1+\mathbf a_2+\mathbf a_3)\) and derive the extra factor for diamond. Which all-even reflections are extinguished?

  5. What Diffraction Does and Does Not Measure. State precisely what information is obtained from (a) Bragg-peak positions and (b) Bragg-peak intensities. Then explain the phase problem in one paragraph.
