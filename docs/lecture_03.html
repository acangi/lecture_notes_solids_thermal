3.2 From Translational Order to Crystal Structure

A crystalline solid is characterized by discrete translational symmetry in three linearly independent directions. Choosing primitive vectors \(\mathbf a_1\), \(\mathbf a_2\), and \(\mathbf a_3\), every lattice translation is

\[ \mathbf R_n = n_1 \mathbf a_1 + n_2 \mathbf a_2 + n_3 \mathbf a_3, \qquad n_1,n_2,n_3 \in \mathbb Z . \]

The set of all points \(\mathbf R_n\) is the Bravais lattice.

The Bravais lattice is deliberately empty: it records only the translational skeleton. To obtain the actual crystal, we must attach a repeating group of atoms to every lattice point.

3.2.1 Basis and Atomic Positions

If the basis contains \(p\) atoms at internal positions \(\mathbf r_\mu\) with \(\mu=1,\dots,p\), then the actual atomic positions are

\[ \mathbf r_{n\mu} = \mathbf R_n + \mathbf r_\mu, \qquad \mu = 1,\dots,p . \]

Thus:

Crystal structure \(=\) Bravais lattice \(+\) basis.

This distinction is fundamental.

  • A monatomic Bravais lattice has \(p=1\).
  • A polyatomic crystal has \(p>1\).
  • A crystal can be made of a single chemical element and still require \(p>1\); diamond is the standard example.

3.2.2 Interpretation

  1. Same Bravais lattice, different basis \(\rightarrow\) different structures. The translational symmetry may be identical while the local geometry changes completely.

  2. Not every visually regular net is a Bravais lattice. The honeycomb arrangement is not a Bravais lattice; it is a triangular Bravais lattice plus a two-point basis.

  3. Chemistry alone does not determine the Bravais lattice. The same stoichiometry \(AB\) can appear in different prototype structures.

3.3 Primitive, Conventional, and Wigner–Seitz Cells

3.3.1 Primitive Cell

A primitive cell is any region of space that contains exactly one lattice point and tiles all space by translation through the Bravais-lattice vectors.

The primitive cell is not unique. The simplest algebraic choice is often the parallelepiped spanned by \(\mathbf a_1\), \(\mathbf a_2\), and \(\mathbf a_3\).

3.3.2 Wigner–Seitz Cell

A particularly symmetric primitive cell is obtained by Dirichlet construction: the region closer to a given lattice point than to any other. This is the Wigner–Seitz cell.

It is often the best cell for visualizing local coordination, even though it is geometrically less simple than the primitive parallelepiped.

3.3.3 Conventional Cell

A conventional cell is chosen to make the visible symmetry of the lattice manifest. It may contain more than one lattice point and therefore need not be primitive.

This is why BCC and FCC are routinely drawn as cubes, even though their primitive cells are not cubic.

3.3.4 Counting Lattice Points in Cubic Conventional Cells

For the cubic conventional cells, the counting is immediate:

\[ N_{\mathrm{lp}}^{\mathrm{SC}} =8 \times \frac18 =1, \]

\[ N_{\mathrm{lp}}^{\mathrm{BCC}} =8 \times \frac18 + 1 =2, \]

\[ N_{\mathrm{lp}}^{\mathrm{FCC}} = 8 \times \frac18 + 6 \times \frac12 =4. \]

Here \(N_{\mathrm{lp}}\) is the number of lattice points in the conventional cell.

This is the geometric reason for the Pearson symbols

  • SC: \(cP1\) for a monatomic structure,
  • BCC: \(cI2\) for a monatomic structure,
  • FCC: \(cF4\) for a monatomic structure.
Note

Important Distinction. A BCC crystal with one atom type is a Bravais lattice. The CsCl structure is not that Bravais lattice: once the corner and body-center sites are occupied by different species, the translation by half the body diagonal is no longer a symmetry of the decorated crystal.

3.4 Crystal Systems and the 14 Bravais-Lattice Types

Today we organize three-dimensional lattices by the metric relations of the conventional cell. The full symmetry classification will be developed in the next lecture.

Crystal System Metric Relations of the Conventional Cell
triclinic \(a \neq b \neq c\), \(\alpha \neq \beta \neq \gamma\)
monoclinic \(a \neq b \neq c\), \(\alpha = \gamma = 90^\circ \neq \beta\)
orthorhombic \(a \neq b \neq c\), \(\alpha = \beta = \gamma = 90^\circ\)
tetragonal \(a=b\neq c\), \(\alpha = \beta = \gamma = 90^\circ\)
rhombohedral \(a=b=c\), \(\alpha = \beta = \gamma \neq 90^\circ\)
hexagonal \(a=b\neq c\), \(\alpha = \beta = 90^\circ\), \(\gamma = 120^\circ\)
cubic \(a=b=c\), \(\alpha = \beta = \gamma = 90^\circ\)

The seven crystal systems support the 14 Bravais-lattice types once the allowed centerings are included. The centering symbols are:

  • \(P\): primitive,
  • \(C\): base-centered,
  • \(I\): body-centered,
  • \(F\): face-centered,
  • \(R\): rhombohedral.

The physically important point for this lecture is that the same crystal system does not imply the same Bravais lattice, and the same Bravais lattice does not imply the same crystal structure.

3.5 Common Elemental Structure Types in Real Space

The most common elemental structures are a small subset of all possibilities. In particular, the cubic structures \(cI2\) and \(cF4\) and the hexagonal close-packed structures \(hP2\) and \(hP4\) dominate among the elements.

3.5.1 Simple Cubic, Body-Centered Cubic, and Face-Centered Cubic

Structure Pearson Symbol Real-Space Description Coordination Number Typical Lesson
SC \(cP1\) simple cubic Bravais lattice with one-atom basis \(6\) pedagogically simple but relatively open packing
BCC \(cI2\) body-centered cubic Bravais lattice with one-atom basis \(8\) conventional cubic cell is nonprimitive
FCC \(cF4\) face-centered cubic Bravais lattice with one-atom basis \(12\) close packed; conventional cubic cell is nonprimitive

A few essential geometric facts:

  • SC is the simplest translational arrangement, but it packs poorly.
  • BCC can be viewed as two interpenetrating simple-cubic sublattices displaced by half the body diagonal. For the monatomic case this is still a Bravais lattice.
  • FCC can be viewed as close-packed \((111)\) planes stacked in the sequence \(ABCABC\ldots\).

3.5.2 Hexagonal Close Packed

The hexagonal close-packed structure is different in an important way: it is not a Bravais lattice by itself. Rather, it is a hexagonal Bravais lattice plus a two-atom basis.

A convenient description uses fractional coordinates in the hexagonal cell:

\[ \left(\frac23,\frac13,0\right), \qquad \left(\frac13,\frac23,\frac12\right). \]

Its close-packed layers stack as

\[ ABAB\ldots \]

and the ideal close-packing ratio is

\[ \frac{c}{a} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{3}} \approx 1.633 . \]

HCP and FCC therefore share two conspicuous real-space features:

  • coordination number \(12\),
  • equal close-packing efficiency for equal hard spheres.

But they differ in their stacking sequence and hence in their translational symmetry.

Note

Another Important Distinction. FCC is a Bravais lattice. HCP has the same local close packing, but it requires a two-atom basis and therefore is not a Bravais lattice.

Optional / Appendix: Ideal Geometry of the HCP Ratio

Treat the atoms as touching spheres of radius \(r\). In the close-packed basal plane the nearest-neighbor spacing is

\[ a = 2r. \]

The atom in the next layer sits above the center of an equilateral triangle of side \(2r\). The four centers form a regular tetrahedron of edge \(2r\), so the layer spacing is the tetrahedron height,

\[ h = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}(2r)=\sqrt{\frac{8}{3}} r. \]

Since the hexagonal repeat contains two such spacings,

\[ c = 2h = 2\sqrt{\frac{8}{3}} r. \]

Therefore,

\[ \frac{c}{a} = \frac{2\sqrt{\frac{8}{3}} r}{2r} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{3}} . \]

3.6 Common Binary Prototypes: Why Chemistry Does Not Fix the Structure

The same idea now becomes more interesting: once different species occupy different internal positions, the basis becomes physically decisive.

3.6.1 A Comparison Table

Prototype Pearson Symbol Underlying Bravais Lattice Real-Space Occupancy Coordination Structural Lesson
CsCl \(cP2\) simple cubic one species at corners, the other at body center \(8\) and \(8\) different species can turn a would-be BCC geometry into SC + basis
NaCl \(cF8\) FCC one species on FCC sites, the other on octahedral sites \(6\) and \(6\) same Bravais lattice as FCC, different basis and coordination
fluorite \(cF12\) FCC cations on FCC sites, anions on tetrahedral sites \(8\) for cation, \(4\) for anion basis determines which interstices are occupied
diamond \(cF8\) FCC identical atoms on two sublattices shifted by \(\tfrac14(1,1,1)\) \(4\) monatomic chemistry does not imply a one-atom basis
zincblende (sphalerite) \(cF8\) FCC same geometry as diamond, but two different species on the two sublattices \(4\) and \(4\) same translational skeleton, different chemistry and lower symmetry

3.6.2 Cesium Chloride

In the CsCl prototype, the primitive cell is simple cubic with a two-point basis:

\[ (0,0,0), \qquad \left(\frac12,\frac12,\frac12\right). \]

Each ion is surrounded by eight oppositely charged neighbors.

The key lesson is conceptual: the body-center site is not a Bravais-lattice point of the decorated crystal, because translating by \(\tfrac12(1,1,1)\) exchanges Cs and Cl.

3.6.3 Rock-Salt and Fluorite

In rock-salt (NaCl), the cations and anions form two interpenetrating FCC sublattices displaced relative to one another. In the conventional cubic cell, one species occupies corners and face centers, while the other occupies the octahedral sites. The coordination number is \(6\).

In fluorite (CaF\(_2\)), the cations again define the FCC Bravais lattice, but the anions occupy tetrahedral rather than octahedral sites. The cations are eightfold coordinated, while the anions are fourfold coordinated.

These two structures are especially useful pedagogically because they show that keeping the Bravais lattice fixed while changing the occupied interstitial sites already produces a different structure type.

3.6.4 Diamond and Zincblende

Diamond and zincblende are among the clearest illustrations of why the basis matters.

For diamond, the underlying Bravais lattice is FCC, but the crystal requires a two-point basis shifted by \(\tfrac14 a(1,1,1)\). Every atom is tetrahedrally coordinated, with coordination number \(4\).

For zincblende (ZnS), the same geometry is retained, but the two sublattices carry different atomic species.

The two key messages are:

  1. A monatomic crystal may still require \(p=2\).
  2. Replacing the second atom by a different species changes the physical content of the basis without changing the underlying FCC translational scaffold.

3.7 Why the Basis Matters: Prelude to Optical Modes and Selection Rules

Up to this point the basis may look like a bookkeeping device. It is not. It changes the physics.

3.7.1 Basis and Degrees of Freedom

If there are \(p\) atoms in the primitive cell, then at each crystal wave vector the lattice has \(3p\) displacement degrees of freedom. Later, in lattice dynamics, these organize into

\[ 3p = 3 + 3(p-1), \]

that is:

  • \(3\) acoustic branches,
  • \(3(p-1)\) optical branches.

This immediately explains why the basis matters so much:

  • SC, BCC, and FCC with a one-atom basis are the simplest monoatomic cases.
  • HCP, NaCl, diamond, and ZnS all have \(p>1\), so additional relative motions of the atoms inside one primitive cell become possible.
  • Diamond therefore supports optical phonons even though it contains only one chemical element.

3.7.2 Basis, Diffraction, and Selection Rules

The basis also controls how different atoms within the same cell interfere in scattering experiments. Even when two structures share the same Bravais lattice, their reflections need not have the same strengths, and some reflections may cancel altogether.

That is the real-space origin of the selection rules and systematic extinctions that will be formulated more precisely once we introduce the reciprocal lattice and structure factors.

3.7.3 Why Today’s Cell Choices Matter for the Next Lecture

The direct lattice that we defined today is the input for the reciprocal lattice tomorrow. The basis that we introduced today is the input for the structure factor tomorrow. And the number of atoms in the primitive cell that we counted today is the input for optical branches later in lattice dynamics.

So the logic is:

  1. choose the Bravais lattice,
  2. specify the basis,
  3. identify the primitive cell,
  4. only then move to reciprocal space, diffraction, and phonons.

3.8 Bridge to the Next Lecture

Today’s lecture was intentionally kept in real space. We defined the direct lattice, clarified what belongs to the lattice and what belongs to the basis, and learned why conventional cells are often visually useful even when they are not primitive.

In the next lecture we will keep the same structures but change viewpoint:

  • from direct lattice to reciprocal lattice,
  • from planes and spacings to diffraction conditions,
  • from basis positions to interference and structure factors,
  • from translational geometry to symmetry classification.

That shift of viewpoint is precisely why today’s distinctions were necessary. Without a clean real-space description, the reciprocal-space description becomes opaque.

NoteTake-Home Messages
  • A crystal structure is not just a repeating set of atoms; it is a Bravais lattice decorated by a basis.
  • The Bravais lattice captures translational order, while the basis captures what sits inside each primitive repeat unit.
  • Primitive, conventional, and Wigner–Seitz cells describe the same lattice in different ways and should not be confused.
  • BCC and FCC are Bravais lattices, but the cubic cells used to draw them are conventional rather than primitive.
  • HCP has the same close packing as FCC locally, but it is not itself a Bravais lattice; it requires a two-atom basis.
  • The same stoichiometry can occur in different prototype structures, and the same Bravais lattice can support very different crystal structures.
  • Diamond is the canonical example showing that even a monatomic crystal can require a multi-atom basis.
  • The number and arrangement of atoms in the basis control later topics such as optical phonons, diffraction intensities, and reciprocal-space selection rules.
