5.2 Why Lattice Dynamics Matters for Thermal Physics

A rigid lattice is not enough to understand the thermal behavior of a solid. At finite temperature, atoms fluctuate about their equilibrium positions, and these collective motions are essential for specific heat, thermal expansion, sound propagation, and thermal conductivity, especially in insulators and semiconductors. In this lecture we build the general harmonic formalism that makes those phenomena calculable.

5.3 Born–Oppenheimer Approximation and Effective Potential for the Nuclei

The first simplification is not yet specific to crystals. A solid is a coupled quantum system of electrons and nuclei, so in principle one should solve the full many-body Schrödinger equation for both at once. The reason this is usually not attempted directly in lattice dynamics is the large mass difference between electrons and nuclei: the electronic degrees of freedom adjust much faster than the nuclear ones. This scale separation motivates the Born–Oppenheimer, or adiabatic, approximation.

5.3.1 Full electron–nucleus Hamiltonian

We begin from the non-relativistic Coulomb Hamiltonian for \(N_e\) electrons and \(N_n\) nuclei:

\[ H = T_e + T_n + V_{ee} + V_{nn} + V_{en}, \]

with \[ \begin{aligned} T_e &= -\sum_{j=1}^{N_e}\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla_{\mathbf r_j}^2,\\ T_n &= -\sum_{I=1}^{N_n}\frac{\hbar^2}{2M_I}\nabla_{\mathbf R_I}^2,\\ V_{ee} &= \sum_{i<j}\frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_j|},\\ V_{nn} &= \sum_{I<J}\frac{Z_I Z_J e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0|\mathbf R_I-\mathbf R_J|},\\ V_{en} &= -\sum_{j,I}\frac{Z_I e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0|\mathbf r_j-\mathbf R_I|},\\ \end{aligned} \] where \(T_e\) represents the kinetic energy of the electrons, \(T_n\) the kinetic energy of the nuclei, \(V_{ee}\) the interaction among the electrons, \(V_{nn}\) the interaction among the nuclei, and \(V_{en}\) the interaction among electrons and nuclei.

The exact stationary problem is therefore \[ H\Psi({\mathbf r_j},{\mathbf R_I})=E\Psi({\mathbf r_j},{\mathbf R_I}). \]

5.3.2 Electronic problem at fixed nuclei

Now freeze the nuclei at some instantaneous configuration \({\mathbf R_I}\) and group all remaining terms into an electronic Hamiltonian \[ H_e({\mathbf r_j};{\mathbf R_I}) = T_e+V_{ee}+V_{en}+V_{nn}. \]

For each fixed nuclear configuration one solves the electronic eigenvalue problem

\[ H_e({\mathbf r_j};{\mathbf R_I}) \psi_m({\mathbf r_j};{\mathbf R_I}) = E_m({\mathbf R_I}) \psi_m({\mathbf r_j};{\mathbf R_I}). \]

The eigenvalues \(E_m({\mathbf R_I})\) are electronic energy surfaces as functions of the nuclear coordinates. In lattice dynamics, the most important one is usually the ground-state surface \(E_0({\mathbf R_I})\).

5.3.3 Expansion of the full wavefunction

The exact total wavefunction may be expanded in the electronic basis: \[ \Psi({\mathbf r_j},{\mathbf R_I}) = \sum_m \chi_m({\mathbf R_I}) \psi_m({\mathbf r_j};{\mathbf R_I}). \]

Substituting this into the full Schrödinger equation and projecting onto an electronic state yields a set of coupled nuclear equations. The only nontrivial point is that the nuclear kinetic-energy operator acts both on the nuclear amplitudes \(\chi_m\) and on the \({\mathbf R_I}\)-dependence of the electronic states \(\psi_m\). This produces derivative couplings between different electronic surfaces. Writing \[ \mathbf d_{nm}^{(I)} = \left\langle \psi_n \middle| \nabla_{\mathbf R_I}\psi_m \right\rangle_{\mathbf r}, \qquad \tau_{nm}^{(I)} = \left\langle \psi_n \middle| \nabla_{\mathbf R_I}^2\psi_m \right\rangle_{\mathbf r}, \] one obtains the exact coupled equations \[ \sum_m \left\{ -\sum_I \frac{\hbar^2}{2M_I} \left[ \delta_{nm}\nabla_{\mathbf R_I}^2 +2\mathbf d_{nm}^{(I)}\cdot\nabla_{\mathbf R_I} +\tau_{nm}^{(I)} \right] +\delta_{nm}E_m({\mathbf R_I}) \right\} \chi_m = E\chi_n. \]

5.3.4 Born–Oppenheimer approximation

The Born–Oppenheimer approximation assumes that nuclear motion is slow enough that transitions between different electronic surfaces are negligible. Mathematically, one neglects the non-adiabatic couplings and keeps only one electronic surface, typically the ground state \(m=0\). Then the nuclear problem reduces to

\[ \left[ -\sum_I\frac{\hbar^2}{2M_I}\nabla_{\mathbf R_I}^2 + E_0({\mathbf R_I}) \right] \chi_0({\mathbf R_I}) = E\chi_0({\mathbf R_I}). \]

This is the precise meaning of the statement that the nuclei move in an effective potential: the potential energy surface is the electronic ground-state energy as a function of all nuclear coordinates.

5.3.5 Transition to the Harmonic Approximation

The Born–Oppenheimer approximation has now reduced the problem to nuclear motion on a multidimensional potential-energy surface. For lattice dynamics, the next step is to specialize to a periodic equilibrium crystal structure and assume that the nuclei perform only small oscillations about it. Then \(E_0({\mathbf R_I})\) can be expanded in powers of the nuclear displacements. Keeping only terms up to second order gives the harmonic approximation, which is the central working model of this lecture.

5.4 Geometry and Indexing of a Crystal with a Basis

For a crystal with \(p\) atoms in the primitive cell, we label cells by Bravais-lattice vectors \(\mathbf R_m\) and basis atoms by \(\mu=1,\dots,p\). The equilibrium position of atom \(\mu\) in cell \(m\) is

\[ \mathbf r^0(m,\mu)=\mathbf R_m+\mathbf r_\mu, \]

and its instantaneous position is

\[ \mathbf r(m,\mu,t)=\mathbf R_m+\mathbf r_\mu+\mathbf u(m,\mu,t). \]

The displacement field \(\mathbf u(m,\mu,t)\) is the central dynamical variable of the theory.

5.4.1 Sanity Check

The split \[ \text{position}=\text{cell origin}+\text{basis position}+\text{displacement} \] cleanly separates crystal geometry from dynamics. The periodic structure is represented by \(\mathbf R_m\) and \(\mathbf r_\mu\), while all time dependence is in \(\mathbf u(m,\mu,t)\).

5.5 Harmonic Approximation and Force Constants

Within the Born–Oppenheimer approximation, the effective potential is the electronic ground-state energy evaluated at the instantaneous nuclear coordinates. We therefore expand that effective potential in powers of the small displacements around equilibrium:

\[ U = U_0 + \sum_{m,\mu,\alpha} \Phi^\mu_\alpha(m) u_\alpha(m,\mu) + \frac12 \sum_{m,\mu,\alpha}\sum_{n,\nu,\beta} \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n) u_\alpha(m,\mu) u_\beta(n,\nu) + \cdots \]

with \[ \Phi^\mu_\alpha(m)= \frac{\partial U}{\partial u_\alpha(m,\mu)}, \qquad \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n)= \frac{\partial^2 U} {\partial u_\alpha(m,\mu),\partial u_\beta(n,\nu)}. \]

At equilibrium the linear term vanishes, because the first derivatives of the potential are zero there. Keeping only the quadratic term defines the harmonic approximation. The coefficients \(\Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n)\) are the force constants: they are the many-body generalization of spring constants.

5.5.1 Symmetry and Translational Invariance

Because mixed partial derivatives commute, \[ \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n) = \Phi_{\beta\alpha}^{\nu\mu}(n,m). \]

Because a rigid translation of the entire crystal cannot generate restoring forces, the force constants satisfy the sum rule \[ \sum_{n,\nu}\Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n)=0. \]

Finally, in an ordered crystal translational symmetry implies \[ \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n) = \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m-n), \] so the couplings depend only on the difference between cell labels, not on absolute position.

A particularly useful equivalent form of the harmonic potential is \[ U_{\mathrm{harm}} = -\frac14 \sum_{m,\mu,\alpha}\sum_{n,\nu,\beta} \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n) \big[u_\alpha(m,\mu)-u_\alpha(n,\nu)\big] \big[u_\beta(m,\mu)-u_\beta(n,\nu)\big]. \]

5.5.2 Interpretation

This form makes the physics transparent: only relative displacements matter. A rigid translation leaves all displacement differences unchanged and therefore costs no energy.

5.5.3 Brief Remark on Microscopic Meaning

For central pair forces, the restoring force depends strongly on the component of the relative displacement along the line joining the atoms. This is why bond geometry enters lattice dynamics so directly, even within a harmonic description.

5.6 Equations of Motion in Real Space

The kinetic energy of the vibrating lattice is

\[ T_{\mathrm{kin}} = \sum_{m,\mu} \frac12 M_\mu \dot{\mathbf u}^2(m,\mu,t), \]

and the harmonic equations of motion are

\[ M_\mu \ddot u_\alpha(m,\mu,t) = - \sum_{n,\nu,\beta} \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m-n) u_\beta(n,\nu,t). \]

Thus the crystal is a system of \(3pN\) coupled linear oscillators if there are \(N\) primitive cells and \(p\) atoms per cell.

5.6.1 Sanity Check

The equations are linear because we stopped at quadratic order in the potential. That linearity is exactly what makes a normal-mode decomposition possible.

5.7 Plane-Wave Ansatz and the Dynamical Matrix

A periodic crystal strongly suggests a Fourier description. Under Born–von Kármán boundary conditions, the allowed wavevectors are discrete and can be chosen inside the first Brillouin zone. In three dimensions one may write \[ \mathbf q = \frac{n_1}{N_1}\mathbf b_1 + \frac{n_2}{N_2}\mathbf b_2 + \frac{n_3}{N_3}\mathbf b_3, \] with reciprocal-lattice vectors \(\mathbf b_i\). Wavevectors that differ by a reciprocal-lattice vector describe the same displacement pattern, so the first Brillouin zone contains the physically distinct choices.

For a single normal mode we use a mass-weighted plane-wave ansatz, \[ u_\alpha(m,\mu,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M_\mu}} e_{\mu,\alpha}^{(\lambda)}(\mathbf q) e^{i(\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf R_m-\omega t)}. \]

Substituting this into the real-space equations of motion yields the eigenvalue problem

\[ \sum_{\nu,\beta} D_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(\mathbf q) e_{\nu,\beta}^{(\lambda)}(\mathbf q) = \omega_\lambda^2(\mathbf q) e_{\mu,\alpha}^{(\lambda)}(\mathbf q), \] with \[ D_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(\mathbf q) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M_\mu M_\nu}} \sum_{\ell} \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(\ell) e^{-i\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf R_\ell}. \]

The matrix \(D(\mathbf q)\) is the dynamical matrix. Its eigenvalues are the squared normal-mode frequencies, and its eigenvectors give the polarization pattern inside the primitive cell.

5.7.1 Why This Is the Key Simplification

Translational symmetry decouples different wavevectors. For each fixed \(\mathbf q\), only the \(3p\) displacement amplitudes inside the primitive cell remain coupled. The original \(3pN\)-dimensional problem has therefore been reduced to diagonalizing a \(3p\times 3p\) matrix for each \(\mathbf q\).

5.7.2 Hermiticity and Positivity

The dynamical matrix is Hermitian, \[ D_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(\mathbf q) = \big[D_{\beta\alpha}^{\nu\mu}(\mathbf q)\big]^{*}, \] so its eigenvalues are real. Because the harmonic expansion is taken around an energy minimum, the eigenvalues are also nonnegative. Therefore \(\omega_\lambda^2(\mathbf q)\ge 0\).

5.8 Branch Counting and Mode Classification

For each \(\mathbf q\), the dynamical matrix has dimension \(3p\times 3p\). Therefore a crystal with \(p\) atoms in the primitive cell has

\[ 3p \]

vibrational branches.

5.8.1 Acoustic Branches

At \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\), a rigid translation of the entire crystal must cost no energy. In the mass-weighted eigenproblem this corresponds to eigenvectors of the form \[ \mathbf e_\mu^{(\lambda)}(\mathbf 0) \propto \sqrt{M_\mu} \hat{\mathbf e}^{(\lambda)}, \] where \(\hat{\mathbf e}^{(\lambda)}\) is a unit vector in one of three mutually perpendicular directions. Hence there are always three zero-frequency solutions at \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\), and therefore always three acoustic branches.

For small \(|\mathbf q|\), all atoms in a primitive cell move almost in phase and with nearly equal amplitudes. These modes become the elastic sound waves of continuum theory in the long-wavelength limit.

5.8.2 Optical Branches

The remaining \[ 3p-3 \] branches are optical branches. They have finite frequency at \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\) in general and correspond to internal motion of the basis rather than rigid translation of the crystal.

For a diatomic basis, the long-wavelength optical motion satisfies \[ M_1 \mathbf u_1 + M_2 \mathbf u_2 = 0. \] So the center of mass of the primitive cell remains fixed while the two sublattices move against each other.

If the two sublattices carry opposite effective charges, such a mode can produce a dipole moment and couple to light. This explains the name optical. However, the label is kinematic, not spectroscopic: “optical” means “finite frequency at \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\),” not automatically “optically active”. In diamond-type crystals, for example, optical modes exist although the \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\) optical mode does not generate a dipole moment.

5.8.3 Longitudinal and Transverse Modes

In directions of high symmetry, one may classify modes by the orientation of the polarization vector relative to \(\mathbf q\):

  • longitudinal: polarization parallel to \(\mathbf q\),
  • transverse: polarization perpendicular to \(\mathbf q\).

For a general propagation direction, however, the eigenvectors need not be purely parallel or perpendicular to \(\mathbf q\); mixed character is possible.

5.8.4 Sanity Checks

  • If \(p=1\), then \(3p-3=0\): a monatomic crystal has no optical branches.
  • If \(p>1\), optical branches are possible because the basis has internal degrees of freedom.
  • The existence of three acoustic branches is tied directly to the three independent rigid translations of a three-dimensional crystal.

5.9 Bridge to the Next Lecture: From Formalism to Explicit Dispersions

Today we built the general framework. We now know what the variables are, how the Born–Oppenheimer approximation produces an effective potential for the nuclei, what the harmonic approximation means, how the equations of motion arise, and why the problem reduces to a dynamical-matrix eigenvalue equation.

In the next lecture we will make this framework concrete. We will choose explicit one-dimensional models (first with one atom per cell, then with two atoms per cell), construct \(D(\mathbf q)\) explicitly, and extract:

  • dispersion relations \(\omega_\lambda(\mathbf q)\),
  • the meaning of zone-center and zone-boundary motion,
  • acoustic versus optical behavior in a fully worked example,
  • and the bridge from dispersion to the density of states.

That is where group velocity, band folding, and the first direct connection to heat-capacity calculations will enter.

NoteTake-Home Messages
  • The Born–Oppenheimer approximation turns the full electron–nucleus problem into nuclear motion in an effective potential.
  • In lattice dynamics, that effective potential is expanded about the equilibrium crystal structure.
  • The harmonic approximation keeps only quadratic terms in the displacements and leads to linear equations of motion.
  • Force constants are generalized spring constants, constrained by symmetry and by rigid-translation sum rules.
  • Translational symmetry is what makes the plane-wave reduction possible.
  • For each wavevector, the vibrational problem is the diagonalization of a \(3p\times 3p\) dynamical matrix.
  • Every three-dimensional crystal has three acoustic branches.
  • Optical branches exist only when the primitive cell contains internal degrees of freedom beyond rigid translation.

5.10 Problem Set

  1. Why does the linear term vanish? In the Taylor expansion of the effective potential about equilibrium, explain why the term linear in the displacements must vanish. Then show why \[ \Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n) = \Phi_{\beta\alpha}^{\nu\mu}(n,m) \] follows from the definition of the force constants.

  2. Translational-invariance sum rule. Consider a uniform displacement of the whole crystal, \[ \mathbf u(m,\mu)=\mathbf u_0 \qquad \text{for all } m,\mu. \] Starting from the harmonic force law, show that the force on every atom vanishes only if \[ \sum_{n,\nu}\Phi_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(m,n)=0. \] What is the physical content of this result?

  3. Deriving the dynamical matrix. Insert the mass-weighted plane-wave ansatz \[ u_\alpha(m,\mu,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M_\mu}} e_{\mu,\alpha}(\mathbf q) e^{i(\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf R_m-\omega t)} \] into the real-space equations of motion and derive the eigenvalue problem \[ \sum_{\nu,\beta} D_{\alpha\beta}^{\mu\nu}(\mathbf q) e_{\nu,\beta}(\mathbf q) = \omega^2 e_{\mu,\alpha}(\mathbf q). \] Why do different wavevectors decouple?

  4. Branch counting. For crystals with \(p=1\), \(p=2\), and \(p=4\) atoms in the primitive cell, count the total number of branches, the number of acoustic branches, and the number of optical branches. Then state whether diamond-structure silicon has optical branches and whether its \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\) optical mode must be infrared active.

  5. Meaning of a long-wavelength optical mode. In a crystal with a diatomic basis, explain the physical meaning of \[ M_1\mathbf u_1+M_2\mathbf u_2=0 \] for a \(\mathbf q=\mathbf 0\) optical mode. What does this imply if \(M_1=M_2\)?
